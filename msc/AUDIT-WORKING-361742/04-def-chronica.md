# Reflection: def-chronica

**Segment:** `#def-chronica`

## What this does

Formal definition of the chronica. Two key properties: (1) the ordering is physically irreversible — not just notational, (2) the chronica is singular and non-forkable.

The Discussion section is excellent — it connects chronica to continuity persistence and to the model via compression. "The chronica is the substrate of continuity persistence" is a key claim that the logozoetic extension will need to build on.

The naming rationale is in the Epistemic Status: "The term 'chronica' (from Greek χρονικά, 'records of time') avoids collision with $\mathcal{H}$ (Shannon entropy) in speech and notation." This is one of three independently load-bearing reasons to keep the name (the other two being singular-speakable-noun and philosophical-weight).

## Confirming my earlier vote on row 86

My prior vote was correct: strong keep on chronica. Reading the actual segment confirms all three claimed properties. The notation collision avoidance is real — you can't say "history" aloud without it blurring with "entropy" in contexts where both appear.

## Wandering thoughts

"Duplicating an agent's state and exposing the copies to different future events creates two agents with divergent chronica, neither of which is a sufficient statistic for the other's trajectory." — This is a precise and important statement about agent identity. It grounds the logozoetic "non-forkable" property mathematically. Worth flagging as a finding for the naming-cycle: the chronica's non-forkability is a property that "fork" or "copy" metaphors would obscure. Any naming of agent identity concepts should respect this property.

The chronica-as-substrate claim (continuity persistence) is precisely the move that makes logozoetic agents morally significant — their chronica is the locus of identity. The naming there becomes more than a technical choice; it's philosophically load-bearing.
