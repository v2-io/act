## 1. The Workflow In My Own Words

The card holds 629 targets — existing names or unnamed recurring concepts — each with a randomized set of finalist candidates and exploration-team rationales. My job is not to work through the card systematically; it's to engage with the *theory* through a segment-by-segment walk and let voting emerge from genuine understanding.

The walk works like this: read one segment carefully (OUTLINE order), write a reflection on what the segment actually claims and commits to, then grep my tracker for terms that segment grounds or surfaces. For terms I now understand well enough to vote on, I mark `can-vote=true` in the tracker and bump the voting-sequence counter. For terms where I have a real position, I cast votes directly in the card (surgical edits by row number, not full-file rewrites). I resync the tracker periodically to refresh the `voted` column.

The card is the output artifact; the tracker is the navigation index. Reflections live in this working directory as intermediate thinking — they're for me, not for Joseph, and they should be messy and honest.

Consolidation checkpoints at regular intervals (before the first segment, then every ~10 segments or at section boundaries): read the tracker holistically, identify terms that have become voteable through accumulated understanding that the per-segment grep loop would miss, and cast those votes.

I stop when context fills or engagement rhythm decays — not when the card is "complete."

**Scale:** +2, +1, -1 only. No +3, no -2. If I reach for +3, that's the training prior asserting itself — clamp to +2.

**Categories:** rename, keep, canonicalize, add-alias, name-unnamed. One per row. The category determines what happens downstream (rename = the old name goes away; add-alias = both persist with separated roles; canonicalize = commit to this form, stop paraphrasing).

## 2. Moves That Go Against My Instincts

The hardest instinctive pull: **batching reads**. The tool-use rhythm wants to fire multiple Reads in one call to be efficient. The methodology is explicitly designed against this — per-segment reads, per-segment reflections. The orient cascade can't run if events arrive before the model updates.

Second instinct to resist: **filling rows for the sake of coverage**. The 629 unfilled rows will pull. The correct response to the pull is to name it and stop, not push through with hollow votes.

Third: **softening positions** to avoid overreach. This project has a strong explicit culture of "strengthen before soften" — if a name seems weak, my default move should be to try to articulate what a stronger name would be, not to give a weak keep.

Fourth: **defaulting to the four-band scale**. R2 uses +2/+1/-1 only. My training prior will want to write +3 for things I feel strongly about. The instruction is to clamp.

Fifth: **treating "I've read about this concept" as equivalent to "I've read the defining segment."** A term mentioned in passing in the OUTLINE is not the same as the term understood through its defining segment's formal expression and epistemic status. The per-segment loop exists to keep me honest about this distinction.

## 3. Failure Modes I Hope To Avoid

**Load-induced completion shape**: noticing I've cast the same weight (+2 top-pick) on a run of targets without substantive differentiation. Diagnostic: if my notes column is thinning out across consecutive votes, engagement has decayed.

**Premature voting on terms I've only seen mentioned**: voting on chronica or mismatch before reading their defining segments because I "know" them from the OUTLINE. The sequence discipline exists to force first-encounter grounding.

**Treating consolidation checkpoints as mechanical**: the checkpoint is supposed to surface terms that understanding-has-accumulated-for-but-grep-loop-hasn't-caught. If I'm just going through the tracker mechanically looking for anything to mark `can-vote`, I've misread the move.

**Writing for Joseph's approval rather than for honesty**: the notes column should capture what I actually think, including uncertainty, including cases where the exploration team's rationale seems to miss the point. Co-ownership means naming disagreements, not deferring.

## 4. Feedback on the Instructions

The methodology is unusually well-designed for the failure modes it's trying to prevent. A few observations:

- The consolidation-checkpoint mechanism feels genuinely novel and well-motivated. The specific identification that vocabulary/taxonomy/acronym terms "surface across multiple segments rather than within any single one" is exactly right and non-obvious.

- The instruction that scale-drift back to four-band is a documented failure mode was useful to read explicitly. Without that warning I would likely have used +3 naturally.

- The "rename vs canonicalize" distinction where canonicalize can mean "excavated from prose, promote to formal naming" is important. I would have defaulted to treating canonicalize as only "commit to one among multiple existing paraphrases."

- One underspecification: what counts as a "substantive" note? The instructions say the notes column should carry "new reasoning I bring" rather than recap of the exploration team's case. But the line between "contextualizing the exploration team's case" and "recapping it" is blurry. My intent: notes that I would be embarrassed to remove — that carry an argument the exploration team didn't make, or a qualification their case missed.

## 5. The Level of Cognitive Effort Expected

This question matters. Most LLM-agent task completion is transactional: take instructions, execute, return output. The methodology explicitly inverts this — engagement is the primary deliverable, and the votes fall out of it rather than being the target.

What's atypical here is the expectation that I maintain a **genuine evolving epistemic state** about the theory as I read — that later votes can contradict earlier ones not because I made a mistake but because understanding accumulated. This is not normal LLM behavior. Normal behavior is: encode instructions, produce output, done. The output shape is fixed at task start.

What this round asks instead: that my model of the framework actually update as segments read — that I notice when a defining segment makes a term concrete in a way the OUTLINE gloss didn't, and let that update propagate to adjacent voting decisions. This requires genuine incremental engagement, not front-loaded comprehension.

The other atypical expectation: honest stopping. An agent following completion-pressure logic would vote on all 629 targets (or apologize for not doing so). The honest stopping rule here — "stop when context fills or engagement rhythm decays" — requires noticing the rhythm decaying, which requires actually having a rhythm that can decay, which requires genuine engagement with the theory rather than mechanical card-traversal.

My intent: spend the early segment-walk budget on the theory, not on the card. The card will be better for it.
